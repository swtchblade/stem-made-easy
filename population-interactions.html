Population Interactions

How species in a community interact, compete, and depend on each other to survive.

Big Idea

Populations in a biological community interact and are interdependent. Relationships between predators and prey, competition for resources, symbiosis, and the niche of each organism all affect survival.

Key Vocabulary

  • Population — a group of the same species living in the same area at the same time
  • Community — all the different populations living in the same area
  • Predator — an animal that hunts and eats other animals
  • Prey — an animal that is hunted and eaten by a predator
  • Competition — when two or more organisms need the same limited resource (food, water, space, mates)
  • Cooperation — when organisms work together for a shared benefit (e.g., a wolf pack hunting)
  • Symbiosis — a close, long-term relationship between two different species
  • Niche — an organism's role in its ecosystem, including what it eats, where it lives, and how it interacts with others

Predator–Prey Relationships

Predator and prey populations are linked. Changes in one population affect the other:

  • When prey increases, predators have more food → predator population grows
  • When predators increase, more prey is eaten → prey population shrinks
  • When prey decreases, predators have less food → predator population shrinks
  • This creates a cycle that repeats over time

These cycles are modeled in food webs, which show all the predator–prey connections in an ecosystem.

Competition and Cooperation

⚔️

Competition

Happens when resources are limited. Two types:

  • Intraspecific — competition within the same species (e.g., two male elk fighting over mates)
  • Interspecific — competition between different species (e.g., lions and hyenas competing for the same prey)

Competition can limit population size and force species to adapt or move.

🤝

Cooperation

Happens when organisms work together to improve survival.

  • Wolf packs hunt together to catch large prey
  • Meerkats take turns standing guard while others eat
  • Ants work as a colony to build nests, gather food, and protect the queen

Cooperation can improve hunting success, protection from predators, and raising young.

Symbiotic Relationships

Symbiosis is a close, long-term relationship between two different species. There are three main types:

🌸

Mutualism (+/+)

Both species benefit.

  • Bees pollinate flowers while collecting nectar
  • Clownfish live in sea anemones — clownfish get protection, anemone gets food scraps
  • Bacteria in your gut help you digest food and get nutrients in return
🐦

Commensalism (+/0)

One species benefits; the other is not affected.

  • Barnacles attach to whales — barnacles get a free ride and access to food, whale is unaffected
  • Birds nest in trees — bird gets shelter, tree is unaffected
🪱

Parasitism (+/−)

One species benefits (the parasite); the other is harmed (the host).

  • Ticks feed on the blood of a dog
  • Tapeworms live inside an animal's intestines and steal nutrients
  • Mistletoe grows on trees and takes water and minerals

Ecological Niche

Every organism has a niche — its specific role or "job" in the ecosystem. A niche includes:

  • What the organism eats
  • Where it lives (its habitat)
  • When it is active (day or night)
  • How it interacts with other species
  • How it reproduces

No two species can occupy the exact same niche in the same ecosystem for long — one will out-compete the other. This is called the competitive exclusion principle.
