Freshwater Resources

How water shapes the land, supports life, and flows through Virginia's major watersheds.

Big Idea

Freshwater resources influence and are influenced by geologic processes and human activity. Water shapes the land through erosion and soil formation, and the nature of subsurface materials affects groundwater. Weather patterns and human usage affect water quality, supply, and the health of Virginia's major watersheds, including the Chesapeake Bay.

Key Vocabulary

  • Watershed — an area of land where all water drains to the same body of water
  • Water table — the underground level below which the ground is saturated with water
  • Aquifer — an underground layer of rock or sediment that holds groundwater
  • Groundwater — water stored underground in spaces between rock and sediment
  • Runoff — water that flows over land instead of soaking into the ground
  • Karst topography — landscape formed by the dissolving of soluble rocks (like limestone), creating sinkholes, caves, and underground streams
  • Erosion — the wearing away and transport of rock and soil by water, wind, ice, or gravity
  • Deposition — when carried sediments are dropped in a new location by water or wind
  • Tributary — a smaller stream or river that flows into a larger one

Water and Geologic Processes

⛰️

Soil Development

Water is a key agent in weathering rock into soil. Freezing water expands and cracks rock. Flowing water dissolves minerals and carries sediment, building up soil layers over time.

🕳️

Karst Topography

In areas with limestone and other soluble rocks, slightly acidic water slowly dissolves the rock, creating:

  • Sinkholes — depressions where ground collapses
  • Caves and caverns — underground chambers
  • Disappearing streams — water that flows underground

Virginia's Shenandoah Valley has many karst features.

Groundwater and the Water Table

The materials underground determine how water moves and is stored:

  • Permeable materials (sand, gravel) — water passes through easily → good aquifers
  • Impermeable materials (clay, solid rock) — block water flow → water collects above them
  • The water table rises during wet seasons and drops during droughts or heavy pumping
  • Over-pumping groundwater can cause the water table to drop, reducing availability

About 30% of the world's freshwater is groundwater — making aquifer protection essential.

Weather, Human Usage & Water Quality

🌧️

Weather Effects

  • Heavy rain increases runoff and can cause flooding
  • Droughts lower water levels in rivers, lakes, and aquifers
  • Seasonal snowmelt feeds rivers in spring
🏭

Human Impact on Quality

  • Fertilizer runoff → eutrophication and algal blooms
  • Industrial waste → chemical contamination
  • Sewage → bacteria and disease
  • Litter and plastics → long-lasting pollution
🚰

Human Impact on Supply

  • Growing populations demand more water
  • Agriculture uses the most freshwater globally
  • Over-pumping wells lowers the water table
  • Impervious surfaces (roads, parking lots) increase runoff and reduce aquifer recharge

Virginia's Watersheds & the Chesapeake Bay

Virginia has several major watershed systems, most of which drain into the Chesapeake Bay — the largest estuary in the United States.

  • The Chesapeake Bay receives water from six states and Washington, D.C.
  • Major Virginia rivers: James, York, Rappahannock, Potomac, Shenandoah
  • Stream processes (erosion, deposition, flooding) shape the landscape and carry sediment and nutrients to the Bay
  • Excess nutrients and sediment from agriculture and development threaten the Bay's health
  • Conservation efforts focus on reducing runoff, restoring wetlands, and protecting underwater grasses
