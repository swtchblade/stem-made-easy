Ecosystems

How living and nonliving things interact, and how matter and energy flow through nature.

Big Idea

Biotic (living) and abiotic (nonliving) factors work together to shape every ecosystem. Matter moves through ecosystems in cycles, while energy flows in one direction — from the sun through producers, consumers, and decomposers.

Key Vocabulary

  • Ecosystem — a community of living things and their nonliving environment interacting as a system
  • Biotic factor — any living part of an ecosystem (plants, animals, fungi, bacteria)
  • Abiotic factor — any nonliving part of an ecosystem (sunlight, water, temperature, soil, air)
  • Producer (Autotroph) — an organism that makes its own food, usually through photosynthesis (e.g., plants, algae)
  • Consumer (Heterotroph) — an organism that gets energy by eating other organisms
  • Decomposer — an organism that breaks down dead matter and recycles nutrients back into the ecosystem (e.g., fungi, bacteria)
  • Food chain — a simple path showing how energy moves from one organism to the next
  • Food web — a network of connected food chains showing all the feeding relationships in an ecosystem
  • Energy pyramid — a diagram showing the amount of energy available at each trophic level

Producers, Consumers, and Decomposers

🌱

Producers

Make their own food using photosynthesis. They capture energy from the sun and are the base of every food chain.

Examples: grass, trees, algae, phytoplankton

🐾

Consumers

  • Primary consumers (herbivores) — eat plants (rabbit, deer)
  • Secondary consumers — eat herbivores (snake, frog)
  • Tertiary consumers — eat other consumers (hawk, lion)
  • Omnivores — eat both plants and animals (bear, human)
🍄

Decomposers

Break down dead organisms and waste. They recycle nutrients back into the soil so producers can use them again.

Examples: mushrooms, bacteria, worms

Food Webs and Energy Pyramids

A food web shows all the feeding relationships in an ecosystem. It is more realistic than a single food chain because most organisms eat more than one thing.

Energy Flow Rules

  • Energy flows in one direction: Sun → Producers → Consumers → Decomposers
  • Only about 10% of energy is passed from one level to the next
  • The other 90% is used for life processes or lost as heat
  • This is why there are fewer top predators than producers

An energy pyramid shows this — it is always wider at the bottom (producers) and narrower at the top (top predators).

Matter Cycles Through Ecosystems

Unlike energy, matter is recycled through ecosystems. The same atoms are used over and over in different forms.

🌫️

Carbon Cycle

  • Plants absorb CO₂ during photosynthesis
  • Animals release CO₂ during cellular respiration
  • Decomposers release carbon when they break down dead matter
  • Burning fossil fuels also releases CO₂ into the air
💧

Water Cycle

  • Evaporation — water turns from liquid to gas
  • Condensation — water vapor cools and forms clouds
  • Precipitation — water falls as rain, snow, or sleet
  • Collection — water gathers in rivers, lakes, and oceans
🌾

Nitrogen Cycle

  • Nitrogen gas (N₂) makes up 78% of the atmosphere
  • Bacteria fix nitrogen into forms plants can use
  • Animals get nitrogen by eating plants or other animals
  • Decomposers return nitrogen to the soil
