Chemical Compounds

How atoms bond to form compounds and the law of conservation of mass.

Big Idea

Atoms combine through chemical bonds to form compounds. In any chemical reaction, matter is neither created nor destroyed — this is the Law of Conservation of Mass.

Key Vocabulary

  • Compound — a substance made of two or more elements chemically bonded together
  • Chemical bond — a force that holds atoms together in a compound
  • Molecule — the smallest unit of a compound, made of two or more atoms bonded together
  • Chemical formula — a combination of symbols and numbers that shows which elements and how many atoms make up a compound (e.g., H₂O)
  • Chemical equation — a way to describe a chemical reaction using formulas and symbols

Types of Chemical Bonds

🔗

Ionic Bonds

Formed when one atom transfers electrons to another atom. This creates oppositely charged ions that attract each other.

  • Usually between a metal and a nonmetal
  • Example: NaCl (table salt) — sodium gives an electron to chlorine
  • Ionic compounds often form crystals
  • They dissolve in water and conduct electricity when dissolved
🤝

Covalent Bonds

Formed when atoms share electrons with each other.

  • Usually between two nonmetals
  • Example: H₂O (water) — hydrogen and oxygen share electrons
  • Covalent compounds often have lower melting points
  • They do not usually conduct electricity

Law of Conservation of Mass

In a chemical reaction, the total mass of the reactants (starting materials) equals the total mass of the products (ending materials).

This means atoms are rearranged, not created or destroyed.

Balancing Chemical Equations

A balanced equation has the same number of each type of atom on both sides of the arrow.

Example: 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O
Reactants: 4 H atoms, 2 O atoms
Products: 4 H atoms, 2 O atoms ✓
