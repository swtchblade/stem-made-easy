Rocks, Fossils & Earth History

How studying rocks and fossils reveals billions of years of Earth's story.

Big Idea

Many aspects of Earth's history and evolution can be figured out by studying rocks and fossils. Fossils are preserved in sedimentary rocks, and scientists use several methods — including superposition, index fossils, and radioactive decay — to date rocks and events.

Key Vocabulary

  • Fossil — the preserved remains, traces, or impressions of ancient life found in rocks
  • Superposition — the principle that in undisturbed rock layers, the oldest layer is on the bottom and the youngest is on top
  • Cross-cutting relationships — the principle that a feature (fault, intrusion) is younger than the rock it cuts through
  • Index fossil — a fossil from an organism that lived for a short time over a wide area; used to identify the age of rock layers
  • Relative dating — determining whether a rock or event is older or younger than another (no exact age)
  • Absolute (radiometric) dating — using radioactive decay to determine the exact age of a rock in years
  • Radioactive decay — the process by which unstable atoms break down into stable atoms at a known rate (half-life)
  • Half-life — the time it takes for half of the radioactive atoms in a sample to decay
  • Geologic time scale — a timeline that divides Earth's 4.6-billion-year history into eons, eras, periods, and epochs

How Fossils Are Preserved

🦴

Mineralization

Minerals slowly replace the original bone or shell, creating a rock copy. This is the most common type of fossil preservation.

🪲

Amber & Ice

Organisms trapped in tree resin (amber) or frozen in ice can be preserved nearly intact for thousands or millions of years.

🐾

Molds & Casts

A mold is an impression left in rock after an organism dissolves. A cast forms when minerals fill that mold.

🌿

Carbonization

Heat and pressure squeeze out gases and liquids, leaving a thin carbon film on rock. Common for leaves and soft organisms.

👣

Trace Fossils

Not actual body parts — instead, evidence of activity: footprints, burrows, trackways, and fossilized dung (coprolites).

🏔️

Sedimentary Rock

Almost all fossils are found in sedimentary rocks because organisms are buried by sediment before they can decompose.

Dating Rocks and Earth Events

📊

Relative Dating

Tells us the order of events, not exact ages. Methods include:

  • Superposition — bottom layers are oldest
  • Cross-cutting relationships — a fault or intrusion is younger than the rocks it cuts
  • Index fossils — if two rock layers contain the same index fossil, they are about the same age
⏱️

Absolute (Radiometric) Dating

Gives a specific age in years using radioactive decay.

  • Radioactive atoms decay at a known, constant rate (half-life)
  • Scientists measure the ratio of parent atoms to daughter atoms
  • Carbon-14 — used for recent organic material (up to ~50,000 years)
  • Uranium-238 — used for very old rocks (millions to billions of years)

Relative and absolute dating are often used together. Relative dating establishes the order, and absolute dating narrows down specific ages.

Virginia's Geologic Record

Virginia contains rocks and fossils from many different geologic periods:

  • Precambrian rocks are found in the Blue Ridge — some of Virginia's oldest formations
  • Paleozoic marine fossils (trilobites, brachiopods) are found in the Valley and Ridge — Virginia was once covered by a shallow sea
  • Mesozoic dinosaur tracks have been found in the Culpeper Basin
  • Cenozoic fossils (whale bones, shark teeth) are found in the Coastal Plain
  • Coal deposits in southwestern Virginia formed from Carboniferous-period swamp forests
