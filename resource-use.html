Resource Use

How we use Earth's resources, and the environmental and economic trade-offs involved.

Big Idea

Resource use is complex. Every resource has environmental liabilities and benefits. Availability, renewal rates, and economic effects must all be considered. Virginia's resources affect both the state's environment and economy, and all energy sources have trade-offs.

Key Vocabulary

  • Natural resource — any material from Earth that people use (water, minerals, soil, fossil fuels, timber)
  • Renewable resource — a resource that can be replaced naturally in a short time (solar, wind, water, biomass)
  • Nonrenewable resource — a resource that exists in limited amounts and takes millions of years to form (coal, oil, natural gas, minerals)
  • Fossil fuel — nonrenewable energy sources formed from ancient organisms (coal, petroleum, natural gas)
  • Sustainability — using resources at a rate that meets current needs without harming future generations
  • Reclamation — restoring land after mining or resource extraction

Renewable vs. Nonrenewable Resources

♻️

Renewable Resources

  • Solar energy — clean, abundant, but depends on sunlight
  • Wind energy — no emissions, but needs consistent wind; can affect wildlife
  • Hydroelectric — reliable, but dams can change ecosystems
  • Biomass — uses organic materials (wood, crops); can be regrown
  • Geothermal — uses Earth's internal heat; limited to certain locations
⛽

Nonrenewable Resources

  • Coal — abundant, cheap, but major source of CO₂ and air pollution
  • Petroleum (oil) — used for fuel and plastics; risk of spills
  • Natural gas — burns cleaner than coal but still releases CO₂
  • Nuclear energy — no carbon emissions during operation, but produces radioactive waste
  • Minerals/ores — finite; mining causes habitat disruption

Environmental Liabilities & Benefits

Every resource use decision involves trade-offs:

  • Benefits: economic growth, jobs, energy production, improved quality of life
  • Liabilities: pollution, habitat destruction, climate change, resource depletion
  • Availability — some resources are geographically concentrated (oil in the Middle East, coal in Appalachia)
  • Renewal rates — fossil fuels take millions of years to form but are used much faster
  • Economic effects — transitioning to renewables creates new jobs but can affect fossil fuel communities

Virginia's Resources

⛏️

Coal

Found in southwestern Virginia (Appalachian Plateau). Major employer but has environmental impacts including acid mine drainage and landscape disruption.

🌊

Water

Virginia's rivers and the Chesapeake Bay are vital resources. Used for drinking water, fishing, transportation, and recreation. Pollution and overuse are concerns.

🌲

Timber & Agriculture

Forests cover about 62% of Virginia. Timber, farming, and fishing all contribute to the economy but must be managed sustainably.

All Energy Sources Have Trade-offs

No energy source is perfect. When choosing energy sources, we must balance:

  • Environmental impact — emissions, waste, habitat disruption
  • Economic cost — cost to build, operate, and maintain
  • Reliability — consistent output vs. weather-dependent
  • Safety — risks to workers and nearby communities
  • Availability — geographic and resource limitations

The goal is to find the best combination of energy sources that minimizes harm while meeting society's needs.
