Work, Force, and Motion

How objects move, the forces that act on them, and Newton's laws that govern it all.

Big Idea

Objects in motion follow predictable rules. Newton's Three Laws of Motion describe how forces affect the movement of objects.

Key Vocabulary

  • Speed — how fast an object is moving; distance divided by time (s = d/t)
  • Velocity — speed in a specific direction
  • Acceleration — the rate at which velocity changes over time
  • Force — a push or pull on an object (measured in Newtons, N)
  • Net force — the overall force acting on an object after all forces are combined
  • Balanced forces — equal forces in opposite directions; no change in motion
  • Unbalanced forces — forces that result in a net force; cause a change in motion
  • Friction — a force that opposes motion between two surfaces in contact
  • Gravity — a force of attraction between all objects with mass
  • Work — done when a force causes an object to move in the direction of the force (W = F × d)

Newton's Three Laws of Motion

1️⃣

First Law — Law of Inertia

An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion at the same speed and direction, unless acted on by an unbalanced force.

Inertia — the tendency of an object to resist changes in motion. The more mass, the more inertia.

2️⃣

Second Law — Force = Mass × Acceleration

The acceleration of an object depends on the net force acting on it and its mass.

F = m × a

  • More force = more acceleration
  • More mass = less acceleration (for the same force)
3️⃣

Third Law — Action and Reaction

For every action, there is an equal and opposite reaction.

  • When you push against a wall, the wall pushes back on you
  • A rocket pushes gas downward; the gas pushes the rocket upward
  • Action and reaction forces act on different objects

Graphing Motion

Distance vs. Time Graphs

  • A straight diagonal line = constant speed
  • A horizontal (flat) line = no motion (object is stopped)
  • A steeper line = faster speed
  • A curved line = changing speed (acceleration)

Speed vs. Time Graphs

  • A horizontal line = constant speed
  • A line going up = acceleration (speeding up)
  • A line going down = deceleration (slowing down)
