Universe & Space

The origin and evolution of the universe — from the Big Bang to galaxies, stars, and beyond.

Big Idea

The Big Bang theory explains the origin of the universe. Stars, star systems, and galaxies change over long periods of time. The characteristics of objects in our solar system are determined by their composition, and space exploration continues to expand our understanding.

Key Vocabulary

  • Big Bang theory — the scientific explanation that the universe began as an extremely hot, dense point about 13.8 billion years ago and has been expanding ever since
  • Galaxy — a huge collection of stars, gas, dust, and dark matter held together by gravity
  • Light-year — the distance light travels in one year; used to measure vast distances in space
  • Nebula — a cloud of gas and dust in space where stars are born
  • Star — a massive ball of hot gas that produces energy through nuclear fusion
  • Solar system — a star and all the objects that orbit it (planets, moons, asteroids, comets)
  • Asteroid — a rocky object orbiting the Sun, mostly found in the asteroid belt between Mars and Jupiter
  • Comet — a body of ice, rock, and dust that orbits the Sun; develops a glowing tail near the Sun
  • Meteor — a streak of light caused when space debris burns up in Earth's atmosphere (a "shooting star")
  • Dwarf planet — a body orbiting the Sun that is large enough to be round but has not cleared its orbital neighborhood (e.g., Pluto)

The Big Bang Theory

Scientists believe the universe began about 13.8 billion years ago with an event called the Big Bang.

  • All matter and energy were packed into an extremely hot, dense point
  • This point rapidly expanded and cooled
  • As it cooled, particles formed atoms, then stars and galaxies
  • The universe is still expanding today

Evidence for the Big Bang

  • Cosmic Microwave Background (CMB) — faint radiation left over from the early universe
  • Redshift — galaxies are moving away from us; the farther away, the faster they move
  • Abundance of hydrogen and helium — matches predictions from the Big Bang model

Stars and Their Life Cycle

Stars are not permanent — they are born, they change, and they eventually die. The life cycle depends on the star's mass.

Life Cycle of a Star

  • Nebula — a cloud of gas and dust collapses under gravity
  • Protostar — the cloud heats up as it shrinks
  • Main sequence star — nuclear fusion begins (hydrogen → helium); the star shines steadily (our Sun is here)
  • Red giant / Red supergiant — the star runs low on hydrogen fuel and expands
  • Death — depends on mass:
    • Small/medium stars → shed outer layers as a planetary nebula → core becomes a white dwarf
    • Massive stars → explode as a supernova → core becomes a neutron star or black hole

Our Solar System

The characteristics of solar system objects depend on what materials they are made of.

☀️

The Sun

A medium-sized star made mostly of hydrogen and helium. Produces energy through nuclear fusion. Contains 99.8% of the solar system's mass.

🪨

Rocky (Terrestrial) Planets

Mercury, Venus, Earth, Mars — smaller, denser, made of rock and metal. Closer to the Sun.

💨

Gas & Ice Giants

Jupiter, Saturn (gas giants) — mostly hydrogen and helium. Uranus, Neptune (ice giants) — contain ices of water, ammonia, methane.

🌙

Moons

Natural satellites orbiting planets. Made of various materials — rock (our Moon), ice (Europa), or a mix. Some may have subsurface oceans.

☄️

Comets, Meteors & Asteroids

Comets = ice and dust (develop tails near Sun). Asteroids = rocky/metallic (mostly in asteroid belt). Meteors = debris burning in the atmosphere.

🔵

Dwarf Planets

Like Pluto and Ceres — orbit the Sun and are round, but have not cleared other objects from their orbital path.

Space Exploration

Evidence from space exploration has greatly increased our understanding of the universe:

  • Telescopes (Hubble, James Webb) — let us see distant galaxies, nebulae, and exoplanets
  • Rovers (Curiosity, Perseverance) — explore Mars's surface, testing for water and past life
  • Space probes (Voyager 1 & 2) — have traveled beyond our solar system and sent back data
  • International Space Station (ISS) — allows long-term research in microgravity
  • Satellite technology — GPS, weather forecasting, communication, and Earth observation
