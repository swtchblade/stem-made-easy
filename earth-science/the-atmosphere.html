The Atmosphere

Earth's complex, dynamic blanket of gases — critical to life and subject to natural and human change.

Big Idea

The atmosphere is a complex, dynamic system. Its composition is critical to most forms of life. Biologic and geologic interactions change atmospheric composition over both short and long time spans. Natural events and human actions — including economic and policy decisions — can stress the atmosphere's regulation mechanisms.

Key Vocabulary

  • Atmosphere — the layer of gases surrounding Earth, held in place by gravity
  • Troposphere — the lowest layer where weather occurs (0–12 km)
  • Stratosphere — the layer containing the ozone layer (12–50 km)
  • Ozone layer — a concentration of O₃ in the stratosphere that absorbs UV radiation
  • Greenhouse effect — when certain gases trap heat in the atmosphere, keeping Earth warm
  • Greenhouse gases — CO₂, methane, water vapor, and nitrous oxide — gases that trap heat
  • Air pressure — the weight of the atmosphere pushing down on Earth's surface; decreases with altitude

Composition of the Atmosphere

Earth's atmosphere is made up of:

  • Nitrogen (N₂) — ~78% — essential for living things but most organisms can't use it directly
  • Oxygen (O₂) — ~21% — needed for respiration by most living organisms
  • Argon — ~0.9% — a noble gas, chemically inactive
  • Carbon dioxide (CO₂) — ~0.04% — small amount but powerful greenhouse gas; used by plants in photosynthesis
  • Water vapor, methane, ozone — trace gases with significant effects on weather and climate

The atmosphere also contains dust, pollen, volcanic ash, and other particles (aerosols).

Layers of the Atmosphere

🌤️

Troposphere (0–12 km)

Where we live and where all weather occurs. Temperature decreases with altitude. Contains ~75% of the atmosphere's mass.

🛡️

Stratosphere (12–50 km)

Contains the ozone layer, which absorbs harmful UV radiation. Temperature increases with altitude. Jets fly in the lower stratosphere.

☄️

Mesosphere (50–85 km)

Temperature decreases with altitude. Meteors burn up here, creating "shooting stars." Coldest layer of the atmosphere.

🛰️

Thermosphere & Exosphere

Temperature increases dramatically but air is extremely thin. The ISS orbits here. The exosphere gradually transitions into outer space.

Biologic & Geologic Interactions

The atmosphere has changed dramatically over Earth's history:

  • Early atmosphere — mostly CO₂, methane, and water vapor (no free oxygen)
  • Cyanobacteria evolved photosynthesis ~2.4 billion years ago → began releasing O₂ ("Great Oxygenation Event")
  • Plants continued adding oxygen and removing CO₂ over hundreds of millions of years
  • Volcanic eruptions release CO₂, sulfur dioxide, and ash — can temporarily cool or warm the climate
  • Weathering of rocks slowly removes CO₂ from the atmosphere over geologic time

Human Actions & the Atmosphere

🏭

Stressing the Atmosphere

  • Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ → enhanced greenhouse effect → global warming
  • Deforestation removes trees that absorb CO₂
  • Agriculture and landfills release methane
  • Industrial chemicals (CFCs) damaged the ozone layer
  • Air pollution (smog, particulates) harms human health and ecosystems
📋

Policy & Economic Decisions

  • Montreal Protocol — international agreement that banned CFCs; ozone layer is recovering
  • Clean Air Act — U.S. law limiting air pollutants
  • Paris Agreement — global effort to limit greenhouse gas emissions
  • Economic trade-offs: transitioning to clean energy costs money but reduces long-term health and environmental damage
