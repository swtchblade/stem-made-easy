Plate Tectonics

How convection currents drive plate movement, shaping mountains, oceans, and geologic features.

Big Idea

Plate tectonic theory explains Earth's internal and external geologic processes. Convection currents in Earth's interior move plates, creating mountains, ocean basins, earthquakes, and volcanoes. Evidence of these processes is found in Virginia's geologic landscape.

Key Vocabulary

  • Tectonic plate — a large slab of Earth's lithosphere (crust + upper mantle) that moves slowly over the asthenosphere
  • Convection current — circular movement of hot material rising and cool material sinking in the mantle; the driving force of plate movement
  • Convergent boundary — where two plates move toward each other (collision)
  • Divergent boundary — where two plates move apart (spreading)
  • Transform boundary — where two plates slide past each other horizontally
  • Subduction — when one plate slides beneath another at a convergent boundary
  • Pangaea — the ancient supercontinent that existed about 250 million years ago before plates separated
  • Fault — a crack in Earth's crust where blocks of rock have moved

Convection Currents Drive Plate Movement

Plates move because of convection currents in the mantle:

  • Earth's core heats the surrounding mantle rock
  • Hot rock rises toward the surface (less dense)
  • At the surface, the rock cools and becomes denser
  • Cool rock sinks back down toward the core
  • This creates a continuous circular pattern that drags tectonic plates along

Convection currents also influence the distribution of materials in Earth's layers and may affect Earth's magnetic field.

Plate Boundaries

💥

Convergent (Colliding)

Plates move toward each other. Results depend on what's colliding:

  • Ocean–ocean → subduction → volcanic island arc
  • Ocean–continent → subduction → coastal mountains & volcanoes
  • Continent–continent → mountains (Himalayas)
↔️

Divergent (Spreading)

Plates move apart from each other.

  • In oceans → mid-ocean ridges and new seafloor (seafloor spreading)
  • On land → rift valleys (East African Rift)
  • Magma rises to fill the gap
↗️↙️

Transform (Sliding)

Plates slide horizontally past each other.

  • Produces earthquakes along faults
  • No volcanic activity or mountain building
  • Example: San Andreas Fault in California

Mountains and Ocean Basins

Plate interactions create Earth's largest features:

  • Mountain ranges — formed at convergent boundaries (e.g., Appalachian Mountains formed when plates collided hundreds of millions of years ago)
  • Ocean basins — formed at divergent boundaries as plates spread apart and new crust fills in
  • Trenches — the deepest parts of the ocean, formed at subduction zones
  • Volcanoes — often found at convergent boundaries (subduction) and divergent boundaries (mid-ocean ridges)

Virginia's Geologic Landscape

⛰️

Appalachian Mountains

Once as tall as the Himalayas, these mountains formed during ancient plate collisions. Millions of years of weathering and erosion have worn them down.

🏞️

Virginia's Geologic Provinces

  • Coastal Plain — flat, sedimentary layers near the coast
  • Piedmont — rolling hills of metamorphic and igneous rock
  • Blue Ridge — ancient metamorphic and igneous mountains
  • Valley and Ridge — folded sedimentary rock layers
  • Appalachian Plateau — flat layers of sedimentary rock with coal deposits
