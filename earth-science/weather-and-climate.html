Weather & Climate

How the Sun's energy interacts with the atmosphere, oceans, and land to create weather and long-term climate patterns.

Big Idea

Earth's weather and climate result from the interaction of the Sun's energy with the atmosphere, oceans, and the land. Weather involves the short-term redistribution of energy, while climate describes long-term patterns. Extreme imbalances can cause severe weather, and human activity is changing global climate.

Key Vocabulary

  • Weather — the short-term state of the atmosphere at a specific time and place (temperature, humidity, wind, precipitation)
  • Climate — the long-term average weather pattern of a region over 30+ years
  • Air mass — a large body of air with uniform temperature and humidity
  • Front — the boundary between two different air masses (cold front, warm front, stationary front)
  • Convection — the transfer of heat by the movement of a fluid (air or water); warm air rises, cool air sinks
  • Coriolis effect — Earth's rotation causes moving air and water to curve (right in Northern Hemisphere)
  • Jet stream — a fast-moving band of wind high in the atmosphere that steers weather systems
  • Climate change — a significant, long-term shift in global or regional climate patterns

Weather: Energy Redistribution

Weather is driven by the reflection, absorption, storage, and redistribution of the Sun's energy:

  • The Sun heats the Earth's surface unevenly (equator gets more than poles)
  • Warm air rises (low pressure) and cool air sinks (high pressure)
  • This creates wind — air moving from high pressure to low pressure
  • Water evaporates from oceans and land → forms clouds → returns as precipitation
  • Dark surfaces (land, forests) absorb more heat; light surfaces (ice, clouds) reflect more

Predicting Weather

📊

Reading Current Conditions

  • Barometer — measures air pressure (falling pressure → storm coming)
  • Thermometer — measures temperature
  • Anemometer — measures wind speed
  • Hygrometer — measures humidity
  • Changes in these conditions help predict short-term weather
🖥️

Weather Models

  • Computer models use current data to simulate future conditions
  • Satellites, radar, and weather stations provide real-time data
  • Models improve with more data and computing power
  • Forecasts become less reliable beyond ~7–10 days

Severe Weather

Extreme imbalances in energy distribution can lead to severe weather conditions:

🌪️

Tornadoes

Violent, rotating columns of air. Form when warm, moist air collides with cool, dry air. Common in "Tornado Alley" in the central U.S.

🌀

Hurricanes

Large, rotating storms that form over warm ocean water (≥80°F). Powered by evaporation and condensation. Bring strong winds, storm surge, and heavy rain.

⛈️

Thunderstorms

Form when warm, moist air rises rapidly and condenses. Produce lightning, thunder, heavy rain, and sometimes hail or tornadoes.

❄️

Blizzards

Severe snowstorms with strong winds (≥35 mph), low temperatures, and reduced visibility. Caused by cold air masses meeting moisture.

🌊

Floods

Occur when water overflows onto normally dry land. Can be caused by heavy rain, rapid snowmelt, storm surge, or dam failures.

☀️

Droughts

Extended periods with less rainfall than normal. Can lead to water shortages, crop failures, and wildfires.

Climate Change

Changes in the atmosphere and oceans due to natural and human activity affect global climate:

  • Natural causes: volcanic eruptions, variations in solar output, orbital changes (Milankovitch cycles)
  • Human causes: burning fossil fuels (CO₂), deforestation, industrial agriculture (methane)
  • Increased greenhouse gases → enhanced greenhouse effect → global temperatures rise
  • Effects: melting ice sheets, rising sea levels, more extreme weather events, shifting ecosystems
  • Solutions: reducing emissions, renewable energy, reforestation, energy efficiency, policy action
