Matter and Atomic Theory

The history of atomic models and the nature of matter.

Big Idea

All matter is made of atoms. Our understanding of atoms has evolved over time through scientific discovery and improved technology.

Key Vocabulary

  • Atom — the smallest particle of an element that still has the properties of that element
  • Element — a pure substance made of only one type of atom
  • Proton — positively charged particle in the nucleus
  • Neutron — neutral particle in the nucleus
  • Electron — negatively charged particle that moves around the nucleus
  • Nucleus — the dense center of an atom containing protons and neutrons
  • Atomic number — the number of protons in an atom; identifies the element
  • Mass number — the total number of protons and neutrons in an atom

Evolution of Atomic Models

Dalton's Model (1803)

Atoms are tiny, indivisible solid spheres. All atoms of the same element are identical. Different elements have different atoms. Atoms combine in whole-number ratios to form compounds.

Thomson's Model (1897)

Discovered the electron. Proposed the "plum pudding" model — negatively charged electrons are embedded in a positively charged sphere, like fruit in a pudding.

Rutherford's Model (1911)

Discovered the nucleus through the gold foil experiment. Atoms are mostly empty space with a dense, positively charged nucleus at the center. Electrons orbit the nucleus.

Bohr's Model (1913)

Electrons travel in specific energy levels (orbits) around the nucleus — like planets orbiting the sun. Electrons can jump between levels by absorbing or releasing energy.

Electron Cloud Model (Modern)

Electrons do not follow fixed paths. Instead, they exist in a cloud of probability around the nucleus. We can predict where electrons are most likely to be found, but not their exact location.

Kinetic Molecular Theory

The Kinetic Molecular Theory explains the behavior of matter in different states:

  • All matter is made of particles (atoms or molecules).
  • These particles are in constant motion.
  • Particles move faster when temperature increases.

States of Matter

  • Solid — particles are tightly packed and vibrate in place. Definite shape and volume.
  • Liquid — particles are close together but can slide past each other. Definite volume, but takes the shape of its container.
  • Gas — particles are far apart and move freely. No definite shape or volume.
