Minerals

The building blocks of rocks — how to identify, classify, and understand minerals.

Big Idea

There are major rock-forming and ore minerals. Their physical and chemical properties help us identify them, determine their uses, and understand how they form.

Key Vocabulary

  • Mineral — a naturally occurring, inorganic solid with a definite chemical composition and crystal structure
  • Luster — the way light reflects off a mineral's surface (metallic or nonmetallic)
  • Hardness — how easily a mineral can be scratched; measured on the Mohs scale (1–10)
  • Streak — the color of a mineral's powder when rubbed on a porcelain plate
  • Cleavage — a mineral breaking along flat, smooth planes
  • Fracture — a mineral breaking with rough or uneven surfaces
  • Crystal structure — the orderly, repeating pattern of atoms in a mineral
  • Ore — a mineral or rock from which a useful metal or material can be extracted profitably

Identifying Minerals

Minerals are identified by testing their physical and chemical properties:

💎

Luster

Metallic — shiny like metal (pyrite, galena)
Nonmetallic — glassy, waxy, pearly, dull, or earthy (quartz, talc)

🔨

Hardness (Mohs Scale)

1 = softest (talc), 10 = hardest (diamond). A mineral can scratch anything softer than itself. A fingernail ≈ 2.5, a penny ≈ 3.5, glass ≈ 5.5.

🎨

Streak

The color of the mineral's powder. This is more reliable than the mineral's outer color, which can vary.

✂️

Cleavage & Fracture

Cleavage = breaks in smooth, flat planes. Fracture = breaks unevenly or with rough edges.

🧪

Acid Test

Some minerals react with hydrochloric acid. Calcite (CaCO₃) fizzes when acid is applied.

🌈

Color & Other Properties

Color alone is not reliable — many minerals share colors. Other tests: magnetism (magnetite), taste (halite = salty), smell (sulfur).

Uses of Minerals

A mineral's characteristics determine its uses:

  • Quartz — very hard (7); used in electronics, glass, and watches
  • Feldspar — most common mineral in Earth's crust; used in ceramics and glass
  • Calcite — main mineral in limestone; used in cement and building materials
  • Hematite — iron ore; used to make steel
  • Galena — lead ore; used in batteries and electronics
  • Talc — softest mineral (1); used in talcum powder and cosmetics
  • Diamond — hardest mineral (10); used in cutting tools and jewelry
  • Halite — table salt (NaCl); used in food, road de-icing, and chemical manufacturing

How Minerals Form

🌋

From Magma & Lava

Minerals crystallize as molten rock cools. Slow cooling (underground) = large crystals. Fast cooling (surface) = small crystals.

💧

From Solutions

When water evaporates, dissolved minerals are left behind and form crystals. Examples: halite, gypsum (evaporites).

🔥

From Heat & Pressure

Existing minerals can be transformed by extreme heat and pressure deep in Earth's crust, forming new minerals (metamorphism).
