Evolution & Natural Selection

How populations of organisms change over time and the evidence that supports it.

Big Idea

Populations of organisms change over time. Mutation, adaptation, natural selection, and extinction drive these changes. The fossil record, genetic information, and anatomical comparisons provide evidence for evolution. Environmental factors and genetic variation influence the survivability and diversity of organisms.

Key Vocabulary

  • Evolution — the change in inherited traits of a population over many generations
  • Natural selection — the process where organisms with traits better suited to their environment are more likely to survive and reproduce
  • Mutation — a random change in DNA that may create a new trait
  • Adaptation — a trait that improves an organism's ability to survive in its environment
  • Extinction — when the last individual of a species dies and the species is gone forever
  • Variation — differences in traits among individuals of the same species
  • Fossil — preserved remains or traces of an organism from the past
  • Homologous structures — body parts that are similar in structure across different species, suggesting a common ancestor
  • Vestigial structures — body parts that no longer serve a useful function but were important in an ancestor

How Natural Selection Works

Charles Darwin described natural selection in four steps:

  • Variation — individuals in a population have different traits (e.g., some moths are dark, some are light)
  • Overproduction — organisms produce more offspring than can survive
  • Competition — individuals compete for limited resources (food, water, shelter)
  • Survival of the fittest — individuals with traits best suited to the environment are more likely to survive and reproduce

Over many generations, the helpful traits become more common in the population. This is evolution by natural selection.

Example — Peppered Moths: Before the Industrial Revolution, most peppered moths were light-colored (to blend in with light tree bark). As pollution darkened the trees, dark moths survived better because they were harder for birds to see. Over time, the population shifted to mostly dark moths.

What Drives Population Change

🧬

Mutation

A random change in DNA that may produce a new trait. Most mutations have no effect, some are harmful, but a few are helpful.

Helpful mutations give organisms an advantage and can spread through a population over time.

🦎

Adaptation

Traits that help organisms survive and reproduce in their environment. Over many generations, natural selection favors these traits.

Examples: a giraffe's long neck for reaching tall trees, a cactus's thick stem for storing water

🔄

Natural Selection

Organisms best suited to their environment survive longer and reproduce more. Their traits are passed to the next generation.

💀

Extinction

When a species cannot adapt to environmental changes fast enough, it may go extinct. Over 99% of all species that ever lived are now extinct.

Causes: habitat loss, climate change, competition, catastrophic events

Evidence for Evolution

🦴

Fossil Record

  • Fossils show how organisms have changed over time
  • Older fossils are found in deeper rock layers
  • Transitional fossils show intermediate forms (e.g., Tiktaalik — fish to land animal)
  • The fossil record shows that many species have gone extinct
🧬

Genetic Evidence

  • All living things share DNA as their genetic material
  • Species with similar DNA sequences are more closely related
  • Humans share about 98% of DNA with chimpanzees
  • Even distant species (like humans and bananas) share some genes
🦠

Anatomical Comparisons

  • Homologous structures — similar bones in different species (human arm, whale flipper, bat wing) suggest a common ancestor
  • Vestigial structures — body parts with no current function (human appendix, whale hip bones) are evidence of evolutionary change
  • Embryology — similar early development in different vertebrates suggests shared ancestry

Genetic Variation and Environment

Genetic variation is essential for the survival of a species. It ensures that some individuals may have traits that help them survive if the environment changes.

  • Populations with high genetic variation are more likely to survive changes
  • Populations with low genetic variation are at greater risk of extinction
  • Environmental factors (climate, food supply, predators, disease) determine which traits are most useful
  • When the environment changes, different traits may become advantageous → the population evolves

Genetic variation comes from mutations, meiosis (shuffling of chromosomes), and sexual reproduction (combining genes from two parents).
