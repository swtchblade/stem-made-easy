Human Impact on Ecosystems

How human activity changes habitats, disrupts ecosystems, and what we can do about it.

Big Idea

Relationships exist between ecosystem dynamics and human activity. Changes in habitat can disturb populations, disrupt species competition, and alter biotic and abiotic factors in ways that reshape ecosystems.

Key Vocabulary

  • Habitat destruction — when human actions remove or damage the natural environment where organisms live
  • Deforestation — clearing large areas of forest, usually for farming or development
  • Pollution — adding harmful substances to the environment (air, water, or soil)
  • Invasive species — a non-native species introduced to an area, often out-competing native species
  • Endangered species — a species at risk of becoming extinct
  • Extinction — when the last member of a species dies and the species is gone forever
  • Conservation — protecting and managing natural resources and ecosystems
  • Sustainability — using resources in a way that meets current needs without harming future generations

Changes in Habitat Disturb Populations

When humans change or destroy habitats, the organisms living there are directly affected:

  • Deforestation removes the homes and food sources of thousands of species
  • Urbanization (building cities) replaces natural land with roads, buildings, and parking lots
  • Agriculture converts ecosystems into farmland, reducing biodiversity
  • Damming rivers blocks migration routes for fish like salmon
  • Mining destroys land surfaces and can contaminate water supplies

When organisms lose their habitat, they must move, adapt, or face extinction.

Disruptions in Species Competition

🐍

Invasive Species

When humans introduce a non-native species, it can disrupt the balance of the ecosystem:

  • Invasive species often have no natural predators in their new home
  • They out-compete native species for food, water, and space
  • They can cause native species to decline or go extinct

Examples: Burmese pythons in the Florida Everglades, kudzu vine, zebra mussels

🐺

Removing Key Species

When humans remove a key species (like a top predator), other populations can explode:

  • Removing wolves from Yellowstone → deer population exploded → overgrazing damaged plants and rivers
  • Overfishing sharks → smaller fish multiply → coral reefs are damaged
  • These chain reactions are called trophic cascades

Changing Biotic and Abiotic Factors

🏭

Air Pollution

  • Burning fossil fuels releases CO₂ and other gases
  • Increased CO₂ contributes to climate change
  • Smog and acid rain damage plants and wildlife
🚰

Water Pollution

  • Chemicals, plastics, and sewage enter waterways
  • Fertilizer runoff causes eutrophication and algal blooms
  • Oil spills coat wildlife and poison marine life
🌍

Soil Degradation

  • Overfarming strips nutrients from the soil
  • Pesticides kill helpful organisms like earthworms
  • Erosion removes topsoil needed for plant growth

What Humans Can Do to Help

Humans can also have a positive impact on ecosystems:

  • Conservation programs — protecting endangered species and habitats
  • Reforestation — replanting trees in deforested areas
  • Reducing pollution — using clean energy, recycling, reducing waste
  • Wildlife corridors — creating pathways so animals can safely move between habitats
  • Sustainable practices — fishing, farming, and using resources responsibly
  • Removing invasive species — manually removing or controlling invasive populations
