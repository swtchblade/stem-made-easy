Cells and Life Processes

Big Idea

All living things are made of one or more cells. Cells are the basic unit of life, and they carry out all life processes. This understanding comes from the cell theory.

Development of Cell Theory & The Nature of Science

Key Vocabulary

  • Cell – the smallest unit of life
  • Cell theory – scientific explanation describing cells
  • Microscope – tool used to see tiny objects
  • Scientific theory – explanation supported by strong evidence

Major Contributions to cell theory:

  • Robert Hooke observed cork and named tiny compartments "cells."
  • Anton van Leeuwenhoek saw living cells for the first time.
  • Matthias Schleiden concluded plants are made of cells.
  • Theodor Schwann concluded animals are made of cells.
  • Rudolf Virchow stated that all cells come from existing cells.

The Three Parts of Cell Theory:

  1. All living things are made of one or more cells.
  2. The cell is the basic unit of structure and function.
  3. All cells come from pre-existing cells.

Sidenote: Nature of Science

Cell theory shows that science: builds on past discoveries, changes when new evidence is found, evolves with improved technology

Cell Structure and Organelles Support Life Processes

Key Vocabulary

Organelle – structure inside a cell that performs a specific job

Eukaryotic Cells – cells that have a nucleus and other organelles (plants, animals, fungi)

Prokaryotic Cells – cells without a nucleus and membrane-bound organelles (only cell membrane and ribosome). These are mostly bacteria.

Here are the basic organelles found in all cells.

◆ Nucleus

  • Contains DNA (genetic instructions)
  • Controls cell activities
  • Directs protein production

◆ Ribosomes

  • Build proteins
  • Found floating in cytoplasm or attached to ER

◆ Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)

There are two types:

  • Rough ER – has ribosomes; helps make and transport proteins
  • Smooth ER – makes lipids and helps remove toxins

◆ Golgi Apparatus

  • Modifies, sorts, and packages proteins
  • Sends materials to where they are needed

◆ Vesicles

  • Small membrane sacs
  • Transport materials around the cell

◆ Mitochondria

  • Break down food to release usable energy
  • Often called the "powerhouse" of the cell

◆ Cytoplasm

  • Jelly-like fluid
  • Holds organelles in place

◆ Cell Membrane

  • Surrounds the cell
  • Selectively permeable (controls what enters and leaves)

Why These Organelles Matter

Together, these structures allow cells to:

  • Use energy
  • Grow and repair
  • Make proteins
  • Maintain internal balance (homeostasis)
  • Respond to their environment

Similarities and Differences Between Plant and Animal Cells

Major Differences

🌿 Plant Cells Have:

◆ Cell Wall

  • Rigid outer layer outside the membrane
  • Provides support and shape
  • Made of cellulose

◆ Chloroplasts

  • Contain chlorophyll
  • Perform photosynthesis (make food using sunlight)
  • Found only in plants

◆ Large Central Vacuole

  • Stores water
  • Helps keep the plant firm (turgor pressure)

🐾 Animal Cells:

  • No cell wall → more flexible shape
  • No chloroplasts → must eat food for energy
  • Smaller vacuoles

Animal cells may also contain:

  • Lysosomes (digest waste materials)
  • Centrioles (help during cell division)

Why These Differences Matter

Plants:

  • Make their own food
  • Need strong support (cannot move)

Animals:

  • Must consume food
  • Need flexible cells for movement

Cell Division: Mitosis

Key Vocabulary

  • Cell division – one cell becomes two
  • Mitosis – division of the nucleus
  • Chromosome – tightly coiled DNA
  • Spindle fibers – structures that move chromosomes

Why Cells Divide

Cells divide to:

  • Grow
  • Repair damage
  • Replace worn-out cells
  • Reproduce (in some organisms)

The Phases of Mitosis

1 Prophase

  • The cell prepares to divide.
  • Chromosomes become visible (DNA coils up)
  • Nuclear membrane breaks down
  • Spindle fibers begin forming

2 Metaphase

  • The cell checks that everything is lined up correctly.
  • Chromosomes line up in the middle of the cell
  • Spindle fibers attach to chromosomes

3 Anaphase

  • The cell separates identical DNA copies.
  • Chromosomes are pulled apart
  • Each half moves to opposite sides

4 Telophase

  • Two nuclei now exist inside one cell.
  • New nuclear membranes form
  • Chromosomes begin to uncoil

Cytokinesis (After Mitosis)

  • Cytoplasm divides
  • Two identical daughter cells form

Why Mitosis Is Important

Mitosis ensures:

  • Each new cell has identical DNA
  • Organisms grow properly
  • Injuries heal
