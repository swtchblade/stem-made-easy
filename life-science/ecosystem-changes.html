Ecosystem Changes

How ecosystems, communities, populations, and organisms change over time.

Big Idea

Ecosystems are dynamic — they are always changing. Organisms respond to daily, seasonal, and long-term changes. Environmental changes can increase or decrease population sizes, and large-scale disturbances can reshape entire ecosystems.

Key Vocabulary

  • Succession — the gradual process of change in an ecosystem's species over time
  • Primary succession — begins in a place with no soil (e.g., bare rock after a volcanic eruption)
  • Secondary succession — begins in a place where soil already exists (e.g., after a forest fire)
  • Pioneer species — the first organisms to colonize a new or disturbed area (e.g., lichens, mosses)
  • Climax community — a stable, mature ecosystem that has reached the end of succession
  • Eutrophication — when excess nutrients (like fertilizer runoff) cause algae to grow wildly, using up oxygen and harming aquatic life
  • Limiting factor — anything that limits the growth of a population (food, water, space, disease)
  • Carrying capacity — the maximum number of organisms an environment can support

How Organisms Respond to Change

🌅

Daily Changes

  • Flowers open during the day and close at night
  • Nocturnal animals sleep during the day and hunt at night
  • Body temperature changes between activity and rest
🍂

Seasonal Changes

  • Migration — animals move to find food or warmer areas
  • Hibernation — animals enter a sleep-like state in winter
  • Trees lose leaves in fall to save water (deciduous trees)
  • Animals grow thicker fur in winter
🕐

Long-Term Changes

  • Climate change over decades or centuries
  • Evolution — species change over many generations
  • Habitats gradually shift due to natural or human causes
  • Ice ages and warming periods throughout Earth's history

Changes in Population Size

Environmental changes can cause populations to grow or shrink:

Populations Increase When:

  • More food or water becomes available
  • Predators decrease
  • Conditions improve (better weather, less disease)
  • New habitat opens up

Populations Decrease When:

  • Food or water becomes scarce
  • Predators increase
  • Disease spreads through the population
  • Habitat is destroyed or degraded
  • Natural disasters strike

Every population has a carrying capacity — the maximum size the environment can support. When a population exceeds this, resources run out and the population shrinks back down.

Large-Scale Disturbances

🟢

Eutrophication

When excess nutrients (often from fertilizer runoff) enter a body of water:

  • Nutrients cause algae to bloom rapidly (algal bloom)
  • Algae blocks sunlight from reaching underwater plants
  • When algae die, decomposers use up the oxygen in the water
  • Fish and other organisms suffocate and die (dead zone)
🌡️

Climate Changes

  • Rising temperatures shift habitats and species ranges
  • Glaciers melt, sea levels rise, and coastal habitats flood
  • Coral bleaching happens when ocean water gets too warm
  • Some species cannot adapt fast enough and face extinction
🌋

Catastrophic Disturbances

  • Volcanic eruptions — destroy habitats, trigger primary succession
  • Wildfires — clear vegetation, trigger secondary succession
  • Hurricanes / Floods — destroy habitat and displace organisms
  • Asteroid impacts — caused mass extinctions in Earth's past

Ecological Succession

After a disturbance, ecosystems rebuild over time through a predictable process called succession:

  • Pioneer species arrive first (lichens, mosses, grasses) and begin building soil
  • Small plants and shrubs move in
  • Trees and larger plants begin to grow
  • A climax community forms — a stable, mature ecosystem

Primary succession starts from bare rock (no soil). Secondary succession starts where soil already exists (e.g., after a fire). Secondary succession is faster because soil is already present.
