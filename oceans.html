Oceans

Complex, dynamic systems that drive weather, support life, and shape Earth's surface.

Big Idea

Oceans are complex, dynamic systems subject to both short- and long-term changes. Chemical, biological, and physical processes interact within the ocean. Unevenly distributed heat drives much of Earth's weather. Sea floor features reflect tectonic processes, and human actions — including policy decisions — affect oceans and the coastal zone, including the Chesapeake Bay.

Key Vocabulary

  • Salinity — the amount of dissolved salt in water (average ocean is ~3.5%)
  • Ocean current — a large, continuous flow of water in a particular direction
  • Thermohaline circulation — global ocean circulation driven by differences in water temperature and salinity
  • Upwelling — when deep, cold, nutrient-rich water rises to the surface
  • Continental shelf — the shallow, submerged edge of a continent
  • Mid-ocean ridge — an underwater mountain range where new ocean floor is formed at divergent boundaries
  • Ocean trench — the deepest parts of the ocean, formed at convergent (subduction) boundaries
  • Estuary — a partially enclosed body of water where freshwater and saltwater mix (e.g., Chesapeake Bay)

Chemical, Biological & Physical Changes

🧪

Chemical Changes

  • Ocean acidification — CO₂ dissolves in seawater, forming carbonic acid, lowering pH
  • Salinity varies with evaporation, rainfall, and river input
  • Dissolved oxygen levels affect marine life
🐠

Biological Changes

  • Phytoplankton produce ~50% of Earth's oxygen through photosynthesis
  • Coral reefs support 25% of marine species but are threatened by warming and acidification
  • Marine food webs depend on nutrient availability and water temperature
🌊

Physical Changes

  • Waves, tides, and currents constantly move and mix water
  • Temperature varies by depth and latitude
  • Sea level changes with ice sheet melting and thermal expansion

Oceans Drive Earth's Weather

Unevenly distributed heat in the oceans drives much of Earth's weather and climate:

  • The equator receives more solar energy → warmer water
  • The poles receive less → colder water
  • Ocean currents redistribute this heat around the globe like a conveyor belt
  • Warm currents (like the Gulf Stream) warm coastal areas; cold currents cool them
  • Evaporation from warm ocean water provides moisture for storms and precipitation

El Niño & La Niña

Periodic changes in Pacific Ocean temperatures that disrupt normal weather patterns worldwide — causing droughts, floods, and temperature extremes.

Sea Floor Features

The ocean floor is not flat — it reflects tectonic and geological processes:

  • Continental shelf — the shallow, gently sloping underwater edge of each continent
  • Continental slope — the steep drop-off beyond the shelf
  • Abyssal plain — vast, flat areas of the deep ocean floor
  • Mid-ocean ridges — underwater mountain chains where new crust forms at divergent boundaries
  • Ocean trenches — the deepest points on Earth, formed at subduction zones (Mariana Trench = ~36,000 ft deep)
  • Seamounts — underwater volcanoes that don't reach the surface

Human Impact & the Chesapeake Bay

🏭

Human Impacts on Oceans

  • Overfishing — depleting fish populations faster than they can recover
  • Pollution — plastic waste, oil spills, chemical runoff
  • Climate change — warming waters, rising sea levels, coral bleaching
  • Ocean acidification — excess CO₂ harms shell-building organisms
🦀

The Chesapeake Bay

  • The largest estuary in the U.S. — where rivers meet the Atlantic Ocean
  • Home to blue crabs, oysters, striped bass, and hundreds of species
  • Threatened by nutrient pollution (fertilizer runoff), sediment, and development
  • Policy efforts include limiting nutrient runoff, restoring oyster reefs, and protecting wetlands
  • Economic importance: fishing, tourism, shipping, and recreation
