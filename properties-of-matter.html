Properties of Matter

What makes matter unique — physical properties, chemical properties, and how matter changes.

Big Idea

All matter has physical and chemical properties that can be observed, measured, and used to identify substances.

Physical Properties

Physical properties can be observed or measured without changing what the substance is made of.

Key Physical Properties

  • Mass — the amount of matter in an object
  • Volume — the amount of space an object takes up
  • Density — how much mass is packed into a given volume (D = m/v)
  • Color, shape, texture — observable characteristics
  • Boiling point / Melting point — temperatures at which state changes occur

More Physical Properties

  • Conductivity — ability to transfer heat or electricity
  • Malleability — ability to be hammered or rolled into thin sheets
  • Ductility — ability to be pulled into thin wires
  • Solubility — ability to dissolve in a solvent
  • Magnetism — attraction to a magnet

Chemical Properties

Chemical properties describe how a substance reacts with other substances or changes into a new substance.

  • Reactivity — how easily a substance combines with other substances
  • Flammability — ability to burn in the presence of oxygen
  • Oxidation — reaction with oxygen (e.g., rusting of iron)
  • Acidity/Basicity — how a substance reacts as an acid or base (measured on the pH scale)

Physical vs. Chemical Changes

🔄

Physical Changes

Change the appearance of matter but NOT its chemical composition. The substance is still the same material.

  • Cutting paper
  • Melting ice
  • Dissolving sugar in water
  • Bending a metal spoon
💥

Chemical Changes

Produce a new substance with different properties. The change is usually not easily reversed.

  • Burning wood
  • Rusting iron
  • Cooking an egg
  • Mixing baking soda and vinegar

Signs of chemical change: color change, gas production, temperature change, precipitate formation.
